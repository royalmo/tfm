\chapter{Preference order estimation for games of ordered preference}
\label{ch:estimation}

Chapter~\ref{ch:lex-ibr-ot} assumed that each agent knows the ordered objectives of the others.
This assumption is restrictive in traffic scenes, where vehicles do not explicitly communicate
which objectives they prioritize or how they rank them.
We now consider the inverse problem for games of ordered preference.
Given a short window of observed behavior and a known set of candidate objectives, the task is to
infer the non-ego preference orders that jointly best explain the observed trajectories.
This chapter develops two offline estimators for that problem and then extends the same idea to
an online adaptive setting in which inference and control are interleaved.

The presentation follows the modeling assumptions of the previous chapters.
The dynamics, shared constraints, and objective library are assumed known, while the ordering of
those objectives is not.
This places the estimation problem close to inverse formulations studied in trajectory games
with unknown opponents \cite{liu:2023}, but here the unknown quantity is the discrete
profile of lexicographic orderings. Existing inverse-game methods can recover
latent goals in trajectory games \cite{liu:2023}, reward or cost parameters from
observed interactions \cite{li:2023,wang:2018,yu:2019}, utility functions in
matrix or generalized Nash games \cite{yu:2023,allen:2022}, and latent
game representations through Bayesian encodings \cite{liu:2024}, but they do not estimate
the preference order itself. In games of ordered preference, that
ordering is the key modeling object, since changing the order can alter
behavior even when the objective library remains fixed.

\section{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:inverse-order-estimation}

Let $e \in [N]$ denote the ego player, whose preference order is known to the
planner, and let $[N]^{-e} \coloneq [N] \setminus \{e\}$ denote the set of
non-ego players whose orders must be inferred.
For each player $i \in [N]^{-e}$, the objective library
$\mathcal{J}^i = \{J^i_1, \dots, J^i_{K^i}\}$ is known, but its priority order is not.
A candidate order for player $i$ is a permutation
$\sigma^i \in \mathfrak{S}_{K^i}$ of the objective index set \([K^i]\), where
\(\mathfrak{S}_{K^i}\) denotes the symmetric group on \([K^i]\).
The unknown object is the joint profile
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}
=
\left(\sigma^i\right)_{i \in [N]^{-e}}
\in
\mathfrak{S}^{-e}
\equiv
\prod_{i \in [N]^{-e}} \mathfrak{S}_{K^i} .
\end{equation*}
We denote the number of admissible joint candidate profiles by
\(M \coloneq |\mathfrak{S}^{-e}|\).

For a measured joint state $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_t$ over a receding horizon of length $T$,
we define the associated short-horizon index range
\(\mathbb{T}_t \coloneq t:t+T-1\).
For the fixed and known ego order \(\sigma^e\), a candidate profile
\(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}\) induces the short-horizon prediction
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{\mathbb{T}_t}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
\mathbf{z}^{*}_{\mathbb{T}_t}\!\left(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_t; \sigma^e, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}\right),
\label{eq:inverse_prediction}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{z}^{*}_{\mathbb{T}_t}\!\left(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_t; \sigma^e, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}\right)\)
denotes the joint optimal strategy returned by the forward ordered-preference solve
of Chapter~\ref{ch:lex-ibr-ot} on the horizon \(\mathbb{T}_t\), with the ego order
fixed to \(\sigma^e\) and the non-ego orders fixed to \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}\).
% As in Chapters~\ref{ch:goop} and~\ref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}, this forward solve also depends
% on fixed game data such as the objective parameters \(\theta^i\), but those quantities
% are assumed known and are suppressed in the notation here because the inverse problem
% varies only the preference orders.
Here and throughout the chapter, hats denote quantities observed in the data window,
such as \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_t\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{\mathbb{T}_t}\), while tildes
denote the corresponding short-horizon predictions generated under a candidate
profile, such as \(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{\mathbb{T}_t}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})\).
% The superscript \(*\) remains reserved for the optimal solution of the forward
% ordered-preference solve, as in Chapter~\ref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}.
% Chapter~\ref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}
% uses the plain symbol \(\mathbf{x}_t\) because only the measured forward state is in
% play there, whereas the inverse setting requires a visual distinction between observed
% and predicted quantities.

This prediction step must be evaluated at the profile level, since
estimating one non-ego order while holding the other unknown players outside the
candidate profile would generally score a different game from the one that generated
the observations.

Let $T_{\mathrm{id}}$ denote the identification window.
Given the observed trajectory window
$\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}$,
let
\[
S\!\left(\,\cdot\,; \hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}\right) : \mathfrak{S}^{-e} \to \mathbb{R}
\]
denote a profile-level consistency score.
The inverse estimation problem then consists of selecting the non-ego profile that minimizes
this score,
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e,*}
=
\argmin_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}}
S\left(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}; \hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}\right).
\label{eq:inverse_problem}
\end{equation}

The score function $S$ compares the predicted non-ego motion
$\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^{-e}_{\mathbb{T}_t}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ with the observed
motion $\hat{\mathbf{z}}^{-e}_{\mathbb{T}_t}$.
This mismatch can be quantified, for example, through a $L_1$ distance as in \cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}, or through squared
Euclidean errors on the relevant control and position components over the
identification window.
The specific choices used in this chapter are the baseline score
$S_{\mathrm{base}}$ in \cref{sec:inverse-baseline} and the combined multi-step
score $S_{\mathrm{comb}}$ in \cref{sec:inverse-bayesian}.

The inverse layer does not introduce a new game solution concept.
Its role is to wrap the forward ordered-preference solver in a discrete hypothesis test over
joint non-ego profiles.
Once a candidate profile $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}$ is fixed, the remaining task is to evaluate
how well the resulting predicted motion matches the observations.
The main difficulty is therefore combinatorial. The candidate set grows as the Cartesian product
$\mathfrak{S}^{-e} = \prod_{i \in [N]^{-e}} \mathfrak{S}_{K^i}$, and each candidate requires one or more forward
prediction solves over the identification window.

Under this interpretation, the estimator is best understood as a finite-hypothesis model-selection
procedure. Each candidate profile defines one fully specified forward game, and the inverse task is
to score how well that game reproduces the observed interaction window. This differs from
inverse reinforcement learning in Markov games \cite{wang:2018,yu:2019} and from
inverse utility learning in matrix or generalized Nash games \cite{yu:2023,allen:2022},
where the unknown object is typically a parameterized reward or utility model.

The closest robotics motivation is game-based intent inference, where planners maintain and update
a finite set of latent hypotheses about the other agent \cite{wang:2020}. Here, however,
the latent hypothesis is not a maneuver label or a single intent variable, but a joint profile of
lexicographic orders over a known objective library. That distinction is what makes the search
space finite yet combinatorial, and it motivates the exhaustive profile evaluation introduced next.

\section{Exhaustive order identification}
\label{sec:inverse-baseline}

The most direct estimator tests every admissible non-ego preference profile and keeps the one whose
predicted first control actions are closest to the observed ones.
For each candidate profile $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}$ we solve
the forward ordered-preference problem from the observed state at each time step of
the identification window, compute \(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{\mathbb{T}_t}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})\),
and accumulate the score
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{base}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
\sum_{t=0}^{T_{\mathrm{id}}-1}
\sum_{i \in [N]^{-e}}
\left\lVert
\tilde{u}^i_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{u}^i_t
\right\rVert_2^2,
\label{eq:inverse_baseline_score}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{u}^i_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ is the first predicted control input for player $i$.
The inferred profile is then
\[\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e,*}
= \argmin_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}} S_{\mathrm{base}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}).\]

This baseline is easy to interpret and directly aligned with the receding-horizon control action.
Its main limitation is that it uses only the first control input of each prediction.
As a consequence, distinct preference profiles that induce similar immediate behavior can remain hard to
distinguish.
The method also scales with the Cartesian product of the candidate order sets.

\begin{algorithm}
\KwData{Known ego order $\sigma^e$, non-ego candidate profile set $\mathfrak{S}^{-e}$, observed trajectory $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}$, and receding horizon $T$}
\KwResult{Inferred non-ego preference profile $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e,*}$}
\medskip
\ForEach{$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}$}{
    $S_{\mathrm{base}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) \gets 0$\;
    \For{$t = 0$ \KwTo $T_{\mathrm{id}}-1$}{
        Solve the joint prediction problem with ego order $\sigma^e$ and profile $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}$ from $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_t$\;
        $S_{\mathrm{base}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) \gets
        S_{\mathrm{base}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
        + \sum_{i \in [N]^{-e}}\lVert \tilde{u}^i_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{u}^i_t \rVert_2^2$\;
    }
}
$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e,*} \gets
\argmin_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}} S_{\mathrm{base}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$\;
\caption{Exhaustive baseline for preference-order identification.}
\label{alg:inverse_baseline}
\end{algorithm}

The exhaustive baseline is a useful reference because it isolates 
which candidate profile best explains the control executed at the current receding-horizon step.
In ordered-preference games, however, that information is often insufficient. Distinct profiles can
produce the same first feasible action because shared constraints or dominant high-priority objectives
govern the immediate decision, while differences in lower-priority ordering only become visible after
the state evolves and the horizon is shifted. For that reason, the next section replaces one-step
control matching with a score that also evaluates the short trajectory segment implied by each profile.

\section{Multi-step trajectory matching and Bayesian ranking}
\label{sec:inverse-bayesian}

The baseline in \cref{sec:inverse-baseline} only evaluates whether a candidate profile explains the next
executed control.
A stronger criterion should also account for the short trajectory segment that the candidate profile
predicts after that control is applied.
To that end, we compare the predicted and observed trajectories over multiple future steps.
% The ambiguity that motivates the multi-step loss is illustrated in
% \cref{fig:inverse_ambiguity_identifiability}.

Let $p_t^i = \operatorname{pos}(x_t^i) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ denote the planar position extracted from
the state of agent $i$ at time $t$.
Accordingly, $\tilde{p}_{t+k}^i(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ and $\hat{p}_{t+k}^i$ denote the predicted
and observed positions induced by the predicted and observed state trajectories.
For a plan-matching horizon $H_p \leq T$, the per-step loss of candidate profile
$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}$ is defined as
\begin{align}
\ell^{\mathrm{imm}}_{t,i}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
&\; w_u \left\lVert \tilde{u}^i_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{u}^i_t \right\rVert_2^2
+ w_x \left\lVert \tilde{p}_{t+1}^i(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{p}_{t+1}^i \right\rVert_2^2,
\notag \\
\ell^{\mathrm{tail}}_{t,i}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
&\; \frac{w_p}{\bar{H}_p}
\Bigg(
\sum_{k=1}^{H_p-1}
\left\lVert \tilde{u}^i_{t+k}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{u}^i_{t+k} \right\rVert_2^2
+ \sum_{k=2}^{H_p}
\left\lVert \tilde{p}_{t+k}^i(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{p}_{t+k}^i \right\rVert_2^2
\Bigg),
\notag \\
L_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
&\; \sum_{i \in [N]^{-e}}
\left(
\ell^{\mathrm{imm}}_{t,i}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
+ \ell^{\mathrm{tail}}_{t,i}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
\right)
+ \rho_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}),
\label{eq:inverse_step_loss}
\end{align}
where $\bar{H}_p = \max\{H_p - 1, 1\}$, and empty sums are taken as zero.
The weights $w_u$, $w_x$, and $w_p$ scale the immediate control fit, the next-position fit, and the
continuation fit, respectively.
The term $\rho_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ penalizes optimization failures and large solver residuals.
In the implementation, $\rho_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ is set to a fixed failure penalty when the prediction does not converge
and to a residual-weighted penalty otherwise.
This preserves a preference for candidates that not only match the observed motion, but also produce
numerically consistent ordered-preference predictions.
Geometrically, a candidate profile is preferred when the predicted short-horizon
motion remains close to the observed trajectory, and penalized when the prediction diverges even if the
first action is locally plausible.

Accumulating \cref{eq:inverse_step_loss} over the identification window yields the trajectory score
\begin{equation*}
S_{\mathrm{traj}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) =
\sum_{t=0}^{T_{\mathrm{id}}-1} L_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}).
\end{equation*}
We also retain the control-only score
\begin{equation*}
S_u(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
\sum_{t=0}^{T_{\mathrm{id}}-1}
\sum_{i \in [N]^{-e}}
\left\lVert
\tilde{u}^i_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \hat{u}^i_t
\right\rVert_2^2,
\end{equation*}
which coincides with the baseline score in \cref{eq:inverse_baseline_score}.
After min-max normalization over the candidate set,
\begin{equation}
\bar{S}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}; S)
=
\frac{S(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) - \min_{\boldsymbol{\sigma} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}} S(\boldsymbol{\sigma})}
{\max_{\boldsymbol{\sigma} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}} S(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) - \min_{\boldsymbol{\sigma} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}} S(\boldsymbol{\sigma})},
\label{eq:inverse_normalized_score}
\end{equation}
we combine both criteria as
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{comb}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
\lambda \bar{S}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}; S_u)
+
(1-\lambda)\bar{S}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}; S_{\mathrm{traj}}),
\label{eq:inverse_combined_score}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in [0,1]$ balances immediate control agreement against multi-step trajectory agreement.
The final estimate is chosen by minimizing $S_{\mathrm{comb}}$.
% The one-player diagnostic implementation used in the offline table below falls back to $S_u$ when its
% candidate set has two elements, in order to avoid spurious rescaling effects in nearly symmetric binary
% comparisons.
In binary comparisons, the implementation falls back to $S_u$ rather than
$S_{\mathrm{comb}}$, since min-max normalization can
exaggerate very small score differences into an artificial separation with only two candidates.

Besides the deterministic score above, we maintain a belief distribution over candidate profiles.
This distribution is initialized uniformly, so every candidate starts with probability
$q_0(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) = 1 / |\mathfrak{S}^{-e}|$.
After each observation step, candidates with smaller loss receive larger exponential weight, and the
distribution is renormalized according to
\begin{equation}
q_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})
=
\frac{
q_{t-1}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) \exp\!\left(-L_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) / \tau\right)
}{
\sum_{\boldsymbol{\sigma} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}}
q_{t-1}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) \exp\!\left(-L_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) / \tau\right)
},
\label{eq:inverse_posterior}
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is a temperature parameter.
This belief update is not used directly to choose the final profile.
It serves as a confidence trace, showing whether the evidence concentrates around one candidate or
remains spread across several behaviorally similar profiles.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:inverse_bayesian} implements the resulting estimator in a single sweep over the
identification window. Each forward prediction solve contributes simultaneously to the control-only
score, the multi-step trajectory score, and the belief update, so the ranking and confidence traces
are built from the same sequence of candidate evaluations.

\begin{algorithm}
\KwData{Known ego order $\sigma^e$, non-ego candidate profile set $\mathfrak{S}^{-e}$, observed trajectory $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}$, plan-matching horizon $H_p$, score weight $\lambda$, and temperature $\tau$}
\KwResult{Inferred non-ego preference profile $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e,*}$ and posterior history $q_t$}
\medskip
Initialize $q_0(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) = 1 / |\mathfrak{S}^{-e}|$ for all $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}$\;
\ForEach{$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}$}{
    $S_u(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) \gets 0$, $S_{\mathrm{traj}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}) \gets 0$\;
}
\For{$t = 0$ \KwTo $T_{\mathrm{id}}-1$}{
    \ForEach{$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}$}{
        Solve the joint prediction problem for profile $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e}$ from $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_t$\;
        Compute $L_t(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ from \cref{eq:inverse_step_loss}\;
        Update $S_u(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$ and $S_{\mathrm{traj}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$\;
    }
    Update the posterior over all profiles using \cref{eq:inverse_posterior}\;
}
Normalize scores with \cref{eq:inverse_normalized_score} and form $S_{\mathrm{comb}}$ by \cref{eq:inverse_combined_score}\;
$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e,*} \gets
\argmin_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e} \in \mathfrak{S}^{-e}} S_{\mathrm{comb}}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-e})$\;
\caption{Multi-step trajectory matching with Bayesian candidate ranking.}
\label{alg:inverse_bayesian}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Adaptive online inference and control}
\label{sec:inverse-online}

The offline estimators infer a single non-ego preference profile before a
closed-loop rollout begins.
In practice, however, an ego agent should update its beliefs while the interaction unfolds.
The implementation also includes an adaptive extension in which inference and control are
performed simultaneously.
The closed-loop structure is summarized in \cref{fig:inverse_online_loop}.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \def\svgwidth{0.98\textwidth}
    \subimport{figures/inverse_goop/}{online_loop.pdf_tex}
    \caption{Adaptive online inference and control loop. Observations are accumulated over a short
    window, candidate profiles are re-scored and smoothed, the current profile is updated only when the new
    profile is decisively better, and the ego player replans against the resulting predictions.}
    \label{fig:inverse_online_loop}
\end{figure}

For each non-ego player $i \in [N]^{-e}$, we consider a small candidate set
$\mathcal{C}^i \subseteq \mathfrak{S}_{K^i}$.
The online profile set is
$\mathcal{C}^{-e} = \prod_{i \in [N]^{-e}} \mathcal{C}^i$.
Unlike the offline case, these sets are not full permutation spaces.
In the implementation used here, each $\mathcal{C}^i$ is formed from a nominal order by a small
number of simple perturbations, such as swapping the first two priorities or reversing the list.
The resulting Cartesian product is a small menu of plausible joint profiles rather than the full
permutation space, which keeps the online computations tractable.
Let $W$ be the observation-window length, expressed in executed control steps.
At update index $m$, each candidate profile $\mathbf{c}^{-e} \in \mathcal{C}^{-e}$ is evaluated by
starting from the observed state at the beginning of the current window, rolling out a short
forward prediction under $\mathbf{c}^{-e}$, and matching the predicted non-ego trajectories
against the measurements collected over that same window.
Each online update requires $|\mathcal{C}^{-e}|$ short forward rollouts over the current
window, followed by one additional refresh rollout for the selected profile before the ego replan.
In the benchmarked implementation, these candidate rollouts are intentionally restarted from the
observed window state rather than carrying candidate-local solver state across windows.
Under the tight relaxation budgets used here, more aggressive warm-start reuse was found to alter
the resulting closed-loop trajectories enough that it was not retained in the reported online
estimator.
The raw score of candidate profile
$\mathbf{c}^{-e} \in \mathcal{C}^{-e}$ is
\begin{equation}
R_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e})
=
\sum_{t \in \mathcal{W}_m}
\sum_{i \in [N]^{-e}}
\left(
\omega_u \left\lVert \tilde{u}^i_t(\mathbf{c}^{-e}) - \hat{u}^i_t \right\rVert_2^2
+
\omega_x \left\lVert \tilde{p}^i_{t+1}(\mathbf{c}^{-e}) - \hat{p}^i_{t+1} \right\rVert_2^2
\right)
+
\rho_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e}),
\label{eq:inverse_online_raw}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}_m$ is the current observation window and $\rho_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e})$ again absorbs
penalties for failed or inaccurate prediction solves.

To avoid switching the inferred profile in response to transient variations, we smooth the profile scores
through an exponential moving average,
\begin{equation}
\bar{R}_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e})
=
\alpha \bar{R}_{m-1}(\mathbf{c}^{-e}) + (1-\alpha) R_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e}),
\qquad \alpha \in [0,1),
\label{eq:inverse_online_smooth}
\end{equation}
and only replace the current profile $\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{cur}}^{-e}$ by the best profile
$\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{best}}^{-e}$ if
\begin{equation}
\bar{R}_m(\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{best}}^{-e})
+
\Delta_{\mathrm{abs}}
+
\Delta_{\mathrm{rel}} \left| \bar{R}_m(\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{cur}}^{-e}) \right|
<
\bar{R}_m(\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{cur}}^{-e}),
\label{eq:inverse_online_switch}
\end{equation}
with absolute and relative switch margins $\Delta_{\mathrm{abs}}$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{rel}}$.
The ego agent then plans against the trajectories predicted under the currently inferred profile,
while the horizon recedes exactly as in \cref{alg:ibr}.

\begin{algorithm}
\KwData{Ego player $e$, candidate profile set $\mathcal{C}^{-e}$, window length $W$, smoothing factor $\alpha$, and switch margins $\Delta_{\mathrm{abs}}, \Delta_{\mathrm{rel}}$}
\KwResult{Control sequence for the ego player and inferred profile for the non-ego players}
\medskip
Initialize a candidate profile for the non-ego players and predict their trajectories\;
\For{each control step $t$}{
    \If{an observation window has just completed}{
        Evaluate $R_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e})$ for all $\mathbf{c}^{-e} \in \mathcal{C}^{-e}$ using \cref{eq:inverse_online_raw}\;
        Update smoothed scores $\bar{R}_m(\mathbf{c}^{-e})$ using \cref{eq:inverse_online_smooth}\;
        Update the inferred profile only if \cref{eq:inverse_online_switch} is satisfied\;
        Refresh the predicted trajectories associated with the selected profile\;
    }
    Solve the ego ordered-preference problem against the currently assumed non-ego trajectories\;
    Execute the first control input and recede the horizon\;
}
\caption{Adaptive online preference-order inference and control.}
\label{alg:inverse_online}
\end{algorithm}

The adaptive scheme above is intentionally lighter than the offline estimator of \cref{sec:inverse-bayesian}.
Its first limitation is combinatorial. Rather than scoring the full permutation space, it relies on small
hand-designed candidate sets built from nominal orders and simple perturbations.
Its second limitation is statistical. For the sake of repeated closed-loop updates, the online layer does not
reuse the full score construction of \cref{sec:inverse-bayesian}, but instead adopts a cheaper windowed
trajectory-matching surrogate with smoothing and a switching rule.
The online layer is thus a tractable online approximation of the richer estimator introduced in
\cref{sec:inverse-bayesian}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:inverse-experiments}

This section evaluates the three inference layers introduced in this chapter.
The evaluation focuses on two questions.
First, how much preference-order information is actually present in a short offline observation window?
Second, if a good nominal profile is already available, can the online updater confirm it early enough to support
closed-loop replanning?
To answer them, the exhaustive baseline of \cref{sec:inverse-baseline} and the richer offline diagnostic of
\cref{sec:inverse-bayesian} are first compared on the same controlled inverse task, and the joint online updater of
\cref{sec:inverse-online} is then evaluated in closed loop.
Throughout, the observations are state-action trajectories generated by the centralized ordered-preference solve of
\cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}, so the true non-ego orders are used only for evaluation and are never shown to the inverse method.

\subsection{Experimental setup}

All experiments were implemented in Julia using the codebase introduced in \cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot} and cited in \cite{julia,code}.\footnote{Code for this chapter is available at \url{https://codeberg.org/ericroy/InverseGoopSolver.jl}. Programs 3 and 4 reproduce the offline experiments; program 6 reproduces the online experiment.}
The simulations reuse the same three-player planar double-integrator scenes of
\cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}, including the horizon $T = 12$, game length $40$, turn length $T_l = 3$,
road geometry, and control bounds.
Only the inference-specific settings change here.
The nominal offline identification window was set to $T_{\mathrm{id}} = 3$.
For the multi-step method, the weights were chosen as
$w_u = 1$, $w_x = 2$, $w_p = 0.35$, and $H_p = 3$, with temperature $\tau = 0.1$ and $\lambda = \frac{2}{3}$.
Optimization failures were penalized by a fixed additive term, while successful solves incurred a small
residual penalty.

Besides exact recovery, the experiments report a weighted order-agreement score.
For non-ego player $i$, with true order $\sigma^i$, estimate $\sigma^{i,*}$, and
$K_i$ priority levels included in the score, we define the weighted inversion score $d_{\mathrm{ord}}^i$ as
\begin{equation*}
    d_{\mathrm{ord}}^i
    =
    \frac{1}{Z_i}
    \sum_{a=1}^{K_i-1}
    2^{-(a-1)}
    \sum_{b=a+1}^{K_i}
    \mathbf{1}\!\left[
        \sigma^i(b)\ \text{is ranked above}\ \sigma^i(a)\ \text{in}\ \sigma^{i,*}
    \right].
\end{equation*}
where \(Z_i = \sum_{a=1}^{K_i-1} 2^{-(a-1)} (K_i-a)\).
We then define the weighted order-agreement score $a_{\mathrm{ord}}^i$ as
\begin{equation*}
    a_{\mathrm{ord}}^i = 1 - d_{\mathrm{ord}}^i.
\end{equation*}
The score $a_{\mathrm{ord}}^i$ equals $1$ for an exact match and decreases as higher-priority pairs are inverted.
We average it over the non-ego players and denote the average by $a_{\mathrm{ord}}$.
We also report $r_1$, the fraction of non-ego players whose highest-priority objective is recovered exactly.
For the reduced offline sweeps below, $K_i = 3$ because only the first three priorities are permuted; for the online
results, the score is evaluated on the full candidate order.

Two scenarios similar to those in \cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot} were considered.
In the highway scenario, the ego vehicle had two admissible objectives, reaching its goal and staying on the road.
In the city scenario, a third objective, maintaining a margin from the road edges, was added.
The side vehicles followed richer six-level preference hierarchies that differed across the two environments.
In highway, those hierarchies emphasized making room for the ego vehicle and maintaining speed, whereas in city
they emphasized yielding, staying in lane, and remaining near the lane center.
A full exhaustive offline search over the two six-level non-ego hierarchies would induce
$720 \times 720$ joint profiles per scenario.
For the offline comparison below, both non-ego players are treated as unknown, but only the first three priorities of
each side vehicle are permuted while the lower-priority tail is kept fixed in its nominal order.
This yields six candidates per non-ego player, hence $6 \times 6 = 36$ implied joint hypotheses.
The reduced offline comparison now evaluates those $36$ profiles jointly at each of the
$T_{\mathrm{id}} = 3$ observation starts, for a total of $108$ joint window fits per method.
The control-only baseline rolls each candidate for one executed step, whereas the richer offline score rolls each
candidate for three executed steps and combines immediate control fit with short-horizon trajectory fit as in
\cref{sec:inverse-bayesian}.
These reduced exhaustive sweeps use one relaxation pass per local solve and focus purely on inference quality,
without an additional closed-loop replay stage.

The reported online results use the joint profile implementation described in \cref{sec:inverse-online}.
For each non-ego player, the four online candidates are the nominal environment-default order itself, the order obtained
by swapping the first two priorities, a one-step left rotation, and the reversed list.
The estimator scores the Cartesian product of those sets.
With two non-ego players, this yields $4 \times 4 = 16$ joint hypotheses per update.
Because the nominal orders also generate the benchmark rollouts, the online experiment only tests
local discrimination around a correct prior rather than exhaustive discovery over arbitrary joint
profiles.
With observation window $W = 6$, each rollout performs six online updates at executed steps $6, 12, \ldots, 36$.
Additional robustness batches apply small perturbations to longitudinal spacing, ambulance offset,
and lateral starting position.
Besides identification accuracy, the online runs report three closed-loop diagnostics. These are
minimum clearance over the rollout, an ego-completion score, and the minimum per-player
solve-success rate.
Here, \emph{minimum clearance} is the smallest slack of the pairwise collision-avoidance constraints
along the executed rollout, so values near zero indicate feasible motion close to the safety boundary.
The \emph{ego-completion score} is the final value of the ego's normalized progress-to-goal metric, with
value $1$ meaning the intended maneuver is completed over the rollout horizon. For each player, the
\emph{solve-success rate} is the fraction of local solves in the rollout that return a numerically acceptable
solution; the reported value is the minimum across players.

The main offline limitation is thus not search size or narrow tuning, but the lack of identifying
signal in these short windows.

\subsection{Online Joint Inference and Control}

The nominal online runs address the closed-loop deployment setting.
In both scenarios, the correct joint profile is already selected at the first update, $t_\star = 6$, and no later
switch is triggered, so the online metric attains $a_{\mathrm{ord}} = 1$ in both cases.
In this local setting, the updater confirms the nominal prior early enough for the ego controller to keep replanning.

The two nominal scenarios still differ in an important way.
Under those diagnostics, highway is the easier case, with minimum clearance $0.027$, ego-completion score
$0.926$, and minimum per-player solve-success rate $1.000$.
City is much tighter, with minimum clearance $10^{-6}$, ego-completion score $0.741$, and minimum per-player
solve-success rate $0.625$.

\Cref{fig:online_discrimination} explains why the city case locks onto the correct profile immediately.
Even a single first-priority swap in one non-ego player bends the predicted short-horizon trajectory away from the
observed window enough to create a clear raw-score gap, and the later updates preserve that ordering even as the
interaction approaches the feasibility boundary.

Even in the tighter city interaction, the inference layer still identifies the correct joint profile from the first

The online experiment also shows that identifying the correct preference profile and solving the
resulting closed-loop control problem are not the same issue. The updater can select the right non-ego order even when
the interaction later becomes numerically tighter and the clearance margins deteriorate.

The robustness batches retain the same inference behavior as the nominal runs.
Across four perturbations per scenario, every run recovers the correct joint profile, attains $a_{\mathrm{ord}} = 1$,
and locks on at $t_\star = 6$.
What changes is again the control difficulty.
Highway remains the easier case, with worst-case minimum clearance $0.025$ and minimum solve-success rate $1.000$.
City is much tighter, since even though inference remains exact across the four perturbations, the worst run drops below the
nominal clearance margin and the minimum solve-success rate falls to $0.600$.
Within a local hypothesis set around a good nominal prior, profile identification is robust to
small geometric perturbations, but closed-loop safety margins are not.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:inverse-discussion}

This chapter extends the forward ordered-preference solvers of the previous chapters to the
incomplete-information setting.
The exhaustive estimator provides a reference, the multi-step score indicates when a
candidate profile explains more than the next control action, and the online updater carries the
same idea into closed-loop interaction. Taken together, the experiments show that
preference-order information can be recovered and used online, but only within the
identifiability limits of the available observation window and candidate set.

% The same experiments also make those limits clear.
% Highway remains ambiguous offline even when the identification window is doubled from $T_{\mathrm{id}} = 3$ to

% The online method therefore earns its performance not from exhaustive discovery, but from local discrimination around a
% good prior.
% It also relies on a hand-designed finite candidate set rather than the full permutation space.
% Finally, the robustness batches show that correct local profile identification does not by itself guarantee comfortable
% closed-loop margins in the tighter city geometry.

This offline ambiguity is consistent with the structure of ordered preferences. To identify an order from observations,
it is not enough to watch a player while only its highest-priority objective is active. Informative evidence appears
only when the interaction forces a trade-off and lower-priority objectives start to affect the motion. If a vehicle is
still moving in a regime where several preference orders produce the same locally optimal behavior, then those orders
remain observationally indistinguishable. In the present benchmarks, this is exactly what can happen in the early time
steps. Before the interaction becomes constraining, one cannot reliably tell whether a side vehicle is following the
highway or city ordering from its trajectory alone.

The online result is best understood as local discrimination rather than unconstrained preference
discovery. Its success depends not only on the windowed score, but also on the quality of the
finite candidate set presented to the updater. In the experiments of \cref{sec:inverse-online},
that set is built around a good nominal order and a few nearby reorderings, so the method only
needs to reject plausible local alternatives. If the true order were absent from that set, or if
the nominal prior were poor, the updater could still rank the available candidates consistently and
yet converge to the wrong profile. The online estimator is robust within its local hypothesis set,
but it is not a substitute for exhaustive search over the full permutation space.

Even with those limitations, the chapter shows that inverse games of ordered preference can recover meaningful
preference-order information and use it to support closed-loop interaction when ordered objectives are not known
a priori.
