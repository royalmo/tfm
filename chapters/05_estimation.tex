\chapter{Preference order estimation for games of ordered preference}
\label{ch:estimation}

Chapter~\ref{ch:lex-ibr-ot} assumed that each agent knows the ordered objectives of the others.
This assumption is restrictive in traffic scenes, where vehicles do not explicitly communicate
which objectives they prioritize or how they rank them.
We therefore consider the inverse problem for games of ordered preference: given a short window
of observed behaviour and a known library of candidate objectives, infer the preference order
that best explains the observed trajectory.
This chapter develops two offline estimators for that problem and then extends the same idea to
an online adaptive setting in which inference and control are interleaved.

The presentation follows the modeling assumptions of the previous chapters.
The dynamics, shared constraints, and objective library are assumed known, while the ordering of
those objectives is not.
This places the estimation problem close to inverse formulations studied in trajectory games
with unknown opponents \cite{liu:2023}, but here the unknown quantity is discrete: the
lexicographic ordering itself. Existing inverse-game methods can recover
latent goals or objective functions from behavior \cite{liu:2023,li:2023,liu:2024},
but they do not estimate the preference order or preference relation
between multiple objectives. In games of ordered preference, that
ordering is the key modeling object, since changing the order can alter
behavior even when the objective library remains fixed.

\section{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:inverse-goop}

Consider an agent $i \in [N]$ with objective library
$\mathcal{L}^i = \{\ell^i_1, \dots, \ell^i_{K^i}\}$.
A candidate preference order is a permutation
$\pi^i = [\pi^i_1, \dots, \pi^i_{K^i}] \in \Pi^i$ of that library,
listed from highest to lowest priority, consistent with the notation introduced in
\cref{ch:goop}.
For a measured state $\hat{x}_t$ and an observed slice of the other players' trajectories
$\hat{\mathbf{z}}^{-i}_{t:t+T-1}$ over a receding horizon of length $T$,
a candidate order $\pi^i$ induces the local lexicographic best response
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^i_{t:t+T-1}(\pi^i)
=
\operatorname{LexBR}^i\left(\hat{x}_t, \hat{\mathbf{z}}^{-i}_{t:t+T-1}; \pi^i\right),
\label{eq:inverse_lexbr}
\end{equation}
obtained by solving the same single-player GOOP subproblem used inside lexicographic IBR over time,
as described in \cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}.
The overall estimation pipeline is summarized in \cref{fig:inverse_pipeline}.
Observed trajectories define the data window, each admissible candidate order is evaluated
through the corresponding local GOOP solve, and the resulting predictions are ranked to obtain
the most plausible ordering.
The discrete search space is illustrated in \cref{fig:inverse_candidate_space}, while the
lexicographic cascade used inside each local solve is summarized in
\cref{fig:inverse_lexicographic_rule}.

Let $T_{\mathrm{id}}$ denote the identification window.
Given the observed trajectory of player $i$,
$\hat{\mathbf{z}}^i_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}$,
the inverse GOOP problem consists of selecting the order that minimizes a trajectory-consistency score,
\begin{equation}
\hat{\pi}^i
=
\argmin_{\pi^i \in \Pi^i} S\left(\pi^i; \hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}\right).
\label{eq:inverse_problem}
\end{equation}
In the experiments below, the observations are state-action trajectories generated by the centralized
GOOP solver and therefore act as ground truth.
The inference algorithms themselves, however, only access the resulting observations and not the
underlying preference order.
When direct control measurements are unavailable, the same framework may be used with controls
reconstructed from the state history under known dynamics.

\section{Exhaustive order identification}
\label{sec:inverse-baseline}

The most direct estimator tests every admissible preference order and keeps the one whose predicted
first control actions are closest to the observed ones.
For each candidate order $\pi^i \in \Pi^i$ we solve \cref{eq:inverse_lexbr} at each time step of the
identification window and accumulate the score
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{base}}(\pi^i)
=
\sum_{t=0}^{T_{\mathrm{id}}-1}
\left\lVert
\tilde{u}^i_t(\pi^i) - \hat{u}^i_t
\right\rVert_2^2,
\label{eq:inverse_baseline_score}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{u}^i_t(\pi^i)$ is the first control input returned by the local solve initialized at
$\hat{x}_t$.
The inferred order is then $\hat{\pi}^i = \argmin_{\pi^i \in \Pi^i} S_{\mathrm{base}}(\pi^i)$.

This baseline is easy to interpret and directly aligned with the receding-horizon control action.
Its main limitation is that it uses only the first control input of each local solve.
As a consequence, distinct preference orders that induce similar immediate behaviour can remain hard to
distinguish.
The method is also factorial in the number of objective levels, since it enumerates every admissible
permutation.

\begin{algorithm}
\KwData{Objective library $\mathcal{L}^i$, candidate set $\Pi^i$, observed trajectories $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}$, and receding horizon $T$}
\KwResult{Inferred preference order $\hat{\pi}^i$}
\medskip
\ForEach{$\pi^i \in \Pi^i$}{
    $S_{\mathrm{base}}(\pi^i) \gets 0$\;
    \For{$t = 0$ \KwTo $T_{\mathrm{id}}-1$}{
        Build the local GOOP subproblem for player $i$ with order $\pi^i$\;
        Solve \cref{eq:inverse_lexbr} using the observed state $\hat{x}_t$ and observed other-player slice $\hat{\mathbf{z}}^{-i}_{t:t+T-1}$\;
        $S_{\mathrm{base}}(\pi^i) \gets S_{\mathrm{base}}(\pi^i) + \lVert \tilde{u}^i_t(\pi^i) - \hat{u}^i_t \rVert_2^2$\;
    }
}
$\hat{\pi}^i \gets \argmin_{\pi^i \in \Pi^i} S_{\mathrm{base}}(\pi^i)$\;
\caption{Exhaustive baseline for preference-order identification.}
\label{alg:inverse_baseline}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Multi-step trajectory matching and Bayesian ranking}
\label{sec:inverse-bayesian}

The baseline in \cref{sec:inverse-baseline} only evaluates whether a candidate order explains the next
executed control.
A stronger criterion should also account for the short trajectory segment that the candidate order
predicts after that control is applied.
To that end, we compare the predicted and observed trajectories over multiple future steps.
The ambiguity that motivates the multi-step loss is illustrated in
\cref{fig:inverse_ambiguity_identifiability}.

Let $p_t \in \mathbb{R}^2$ denote the planar position components of the state at time $t$.
For a plan-matching horizon $H_p \leq T$, the per-step loss of candidate order $\pi^i$ is defined as
\begin{align}
L_t(\pi^i)
=
&\; w_u \left\lVert \tilde{u}^i_t(\pi^i) - \hat{u}^i_t \right\rVert_2^2
+ w_x \left\lVert \tilde{p}^i_{t+1}(\pi^i) - \hat{p}^i_{t+1} \right\rVert_2^2 \notag \\
&+ \frac{w_p}{H_p}
\sum_{k=0}^{H_p-1}
\left\lVert \tilde{u}^i_{t+k}(\pi^i) - \hat{u}^i_{t+k} \right\rVert_2^2
+ \frac{w_p}{H_p}
\sum_{k=1}^{H_p}
\left\lVert \tilde{p}^i_{t+k}(\pi^i) - \hat{p}^i_{t+k} \right\rVert_2^2
+ \rho_t(\pi^i),
\label{eq:inverse_step_loss}
\end{align}
where $w_u$, $w_x$, and $w_p$ weight the immediate control fit, the next-state fit, and the short-horizon
plan fit, respectively.
The term $\rho_t(\pi^i)$ penalizes local optimization failures and large solver residuals.
In the implementation, $\rho_t(\pi^i)$ is set to a fixed failure penalty when the local solve does not converge
and to a residual-weighted penalty otherwise.
This preserves a preference for candidates that not only match the observed motion, but also produce
numerically consistent local GOOP solutions.
The geometric effect of this multi-step comparison is illustrated in
\cref{fig:trajectory_matching_schema}: a candidate order is preferred when the predicted short-horizon
motion remains close to the observed trajectory, and penalized when the prediction diverges even if the
first action is locally plausible.

    \label{fig:inverse_ambiguity_identifiability}
\end{figure}

Accumulating \cref{eq:inverse_step_loss} over the identification window yields the trajectory score
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{traj}}(\pi^i) = \sum_{t=0}^{T_{\mathrm{id}}-1} L_t(\pi^i).
\label{eq:inverse_traj_score}
\end{equation}
We also retain the control-only score
\begin{equation}
S_u(\pi^i)
=
\sum_{t=0}^{T_{\mathrm{id}}-1}
\left\lVert
\tilde{u}^i_t(\pi^i) - \hat{u}^i_t
\right\rVert_2^2,
\label{eq:inverse_control_score}
\end{equation}
which coincides with the baseline score in \cref{eq:inverse_baseline_score}.
After min-max normalization over the candidate set,
\begin{equation}
\bar{S}(\pi^i; S)
=
\frac{S(\pi^i) - \min_{\pi \in \Pi^i} S(\pi)}{\max_{\pi \in \Pi^i} S(\pi) - \min_{\pi \in \Pi^i} S(\pi)},
\label{eq:inverse_normalized_score}
\end{equation}
we combine both criteria as
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{comb}}(\pi^i)
=
\bar{S}(\pi^i; S_u)
+
\frac{1}{2} \bar{S}(\pi^i; S_{\mathrm{traj}}).
\label{eq:inverse_combined_score}
\end{equation}
The final estimate is chosen by minimizing $S_{\mathrm{comb}}$.
The role of the individual ranking ingredients is summarized in
\cref{fig:inverse_score_decomposition}.
In the special case $|\Pi^i| = 2$, the implementation falls back to $S_u$ in order to avoid spurious
rescaling effects in nearly symmetric binary comparisons.

In addition to the deterministic score above, we maintain an online posterior over candidate orders.
Starting from the uniform prior $p_0(\pi^i) = 1 / |\Pi^i|$, we update
\begin{equation}
p_t(\pi^i)
=
\frac{p_{t-1}(\pi^i) \exp\!\left(-L_t(\pi^i) / \tau\right)}{\sum_{\pi \in \Pi^i} p_{t-1}(\pi) \exp\!\left(-L_t(\pi) / \tau\right)},
\label{eq:inverse_posterior}
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is a temperature parameter.
The posterior is not used directly to choose the final order.
Instead, it acts as a confidence trace that reveals whether the candidate set is sharply separated or
whether several orders remain observationally similar.
Its evolution over the identification window is shown in
\cref{fig:inverse_posterior_evolution}.

    \centering
    \def\svgwidth{0.98\textwidth}
    \subimport{figures/inverse_goop/}{score_decomposition.pdf_tex}
    \caption{Decomposition of the ranking score. The control-only term, the multi-step trajectory term,
    and the posterior confidence trace are combined to obtain the final order estimate.}
    \label{fig:inverse_score_decomposition}
\end{figure}

\end{figure}

\begin{algorithm}
\KwData{Objective library $\mathcal{L}^i$, candidate set $\Pi^i$, observed trajectories $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_{0:T_{\mathrm{id}}+T-1}$, plan-matching horizon $H_p$, and temperature $\tau$}
\KwResult{Inferred preference order $\hat{\pi}^i$ and posterior history $p_t$}
\medskip
Initialize $p_0(\pi^i) = 1 / |\Pi^i|$ for all $\pi^i \in \Pi^i$\;
\ForEach{$\pi^i \in \Pi^i$}{
    $S_u(\pi^i) \gets 0$, $S_{\mathrm{traj}}(\pi^i) \gets 0$\;
    \For{$t = 0$ \KwTo $T_{\mathrm{id}}-1$}{
        Solve the local GOOP subproblem for order $\pi^i$\;
        Compute $L_t(\pi^i)$ from \cref{eq:inverse_step_loss}\;
        Update $S_u(\pi^i)$ and $S_{\mathrm{traj}}(\pi^i)$\;
        Update the posterior using \cref{eq:inverse_posterior}\;
    }
}
Normalize scores with \cref{eq:inverse_normalized_score} and form $S_{\mathrm{comb}}$ by \cref{eq:inverse_combined_score}\;
$\hat{\pi}^i \gets \argmin_{\pi^i \in \Pi^i} S_{\mathrm{comb}}(\pi^i)$\;
\caption{Multi-step trajectory matching with Bayesian candidate ranking.}
\label{alg:inverse_bayesian}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Adaptive online inference and control}
\label{sec:inverse-online}

The offline estimators infer a single preference order before a decentralized rollout begins.
In practice, however, an ego agent should update its beliefs while the interaction unfolds.
The implementation therefore includes an adaptive extension in which inference and control are
performed simultaneously.
The closed-loop structure is summarized in \cref{fig:inverse_online_loop}.

For each non-ego player $j \neq i$, we consider a small candidate set
$\mathcal{C}^j \subseteq \Pi^j$.
Unlike the offline case, this set is not the full permutation space.
Instead, it is built from a nominal order by a small number of simple reorderings, which keeps the
online computations tractable.
Let $W$ be the observation-window length, expressed in executed control steps.
At update index $m$, the raw score of candidate $c \in \mathcal{C}^j$ is
\begin{equation}
R_m^j(c)
=
\sum_{t \in \mathcal{W}_m}
\left(
\omega_u \left\lVert \tilde{u}^j_t(c) - \hat{u}^j_t \right\rVert_2^2
+
\omega_x \left\lVert \tilde{p}^j_{t+1}(c) - \hat{p}^j_{t+1} \right\rVert_2^2
\right)
+
\rho_m^j(c),
\label{eq:inverse_online_raw}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}_m$ is the current observation window and $\rho_m^j(c)$ again absorbs penalties for
failed or inaccurate local solves.

To avoid switching the inferred order in response to transient variations, we smooth the candidate scores
through an exponential moving average,
\begin{equation}
\bar{R}_m^j(c)
=
\alpha \bar{R}_{m-1}^j(c) + (1-\alpha) R_m^j(c),
\qquad \alpha \in [0,1),
\label{eq:inverse_online_smooth}
\end{equation}
and only replace the current candidate $c_{\mathrm{cur}}$ by the best candidate
$c_{\mathrm{best}}$ if
\begin{equation}
\bar{R}_m^j(c_{\mathrm{best}})
+
\Delta_{\mathrm{abs}}
+
\Delta_{\mathrm{rel}} \left| \bar{R}_m^j(c_{\mathrm{cur}}) \right|
<
\bar{R}_m^j(c_{\mathrm{cur}}),
\label{eq:inverse_online_switch}
\end{equation}
with absolute and relative switch margins $\Delta_{\mathrm{abs}}$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{rel}}$.
The ego agent then plans against the trajectories predicted under the currently inferred orders,
while the horizon recedes exactly as in \cref{alg:ibr}.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \def\svgwidth{0.98\textwidth}
    \subimport{figures/inverse_goop/}{online_loop.pdf_tex}
    \caption{Adaptive online inference and control loop. Observations are accumulated over a short
    window, candidate orders are re-scored and smoothed, the current order is updated only when the new
    candidate is decisively better, and the ego player replans against the resulting predictions.}
    \label{fig:inverse_online_loop}
\end{figure}

\begin{algorithm}
\KwData{Ego player $i$, candidate sets $\mathcal{C}^j$ for $j \neq i$, window length $W$, smoothing factor $\alpha$, and switch margins $\Delta_{\mathrm{abs}}, \Delta_{\mathrm{rel}}$}
\KwResult{Online control sequence for the ego player and inferred orders for the non-ego players}
\medskip
Initialize one candidate order for each non-ego player and predict their trajectories\;
\For{each control step $t$}{
    \If{an observation window has just completed}{
        \ForEach{non-ego player $j \neq i$}{
            Evaluate $R_m^j(c)$ for all $c \in \mathcal{C}^j$ using \cref{eq:inverse_online_raw}\;
            Update smoothed scores $\bar{R}_m^j(c)$ using \cref{eq:inverse_online_smooth}\;
            Update the inferred candidate only if \cref{eq:inverse_online_switch} is satisfied\;
            Refresh the predicted trajectory associated with the selected candidate\;
        }
    }
    Solve the ego GOOP subproblem against the currently assumed non-ego trajectories\;
    Execute the first control input and recede the horizon\;
}
\caption{Adaptive online preference-order inference and control.}
\label{alg:inverse_online}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:inverse-experiments}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

All experiments were implemented in Julia on top of \texttt{LEXIBROverTime.jl}
\cite{julia,code}.
The simulated system uses the planar double-integrator dynamics from the same stack, with three players,
planning horizon $T = 12$, game length $40$, turn length $T_l = 3$, time step $0.1$, road width $1.2$,
collision radius $0.3$, speed limit $3.2$, and acceleration limit $2.0$.
The offline identification window was set to $T_{\mathrm{id}} = 3$.
For the multi-step method, the weights were chosen as
$w_u = 1$, $w_x = 2$, $w_p = 0.35$, and $H_p = 3$, with temperature $\tau = 0.1$.
Optimization failures were penalized by a fixed additive term, while successful solves incurred a small
residual penalty.

Two scenarios were considered.
In the highway scenario, the ego vehicle had two admissible objectives, reaching its goal and staying on the road.
In the city scenario, a third objective, maintaining a margin from the road edges, was added.
The side vehicles followed richer six-level preference hierarchies that differed across the two environments.
In highway, those hierarchies emphasized making room for the ego vehicle and maintaining speed, whereas in city
they emphasized yielding, staying in lane, and remaining near the lane center.
For the offline experiments, the candidate set for the ego player was the full permutation space,
which yields two candidates in highway and six in city.
For the online experiment, each non-ego player was instead assigned a four-element candidate set built from a
nominal order and a small number of order perturbations.

\subsection{Offline preference-order estimation}

\Cref{tab:inverse_offline} compares the exhaustive baseline and the multi-step estimator on the two benchmark
environments.
In both cases the observations were generated by the centralized GOOP solve of
\cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}, after which the inferred ego order was inserted into the decentralized controller and the
resulting rollout was evaluated.
The reported clearance is therefore a closed-loop quantity, not merely a fitting statistic.

The city scenario is identifiable from a short observation window.
Both estimators recover the correct ego preference order, and the decentralized rollout obtained with the inferred
order remains feasible.
The multi-step method does not improve the final order estimate in this case, but it produces a richer diagnostic:
its cumulative trajectory score accounts for consistency over several future steps rather than only the next executed
control.
At the same time, the posterior remains diffuse, with a best probability close to the uniform value $1/6$.
This indicates that several candidate orders remain behaviourally similar over a three-step identification window,
even though the final combined score still ranks the correct order first.

The highway scenario is more challenging.
With only two candidate orders, both methods select the wrong permutation under the current default setup.
The associated posterior is essentially uniform, which suggests an identifiability issue rather than an optimization
failure: the two orders induce nearly indistinguishable short-horizon responses in this symmetric setting.
Even so, the decentralized rollout obtained with the inferred order remains safe, with positive minimum clearance.
This is an important observation for inverse GOOPs.
Preference-order estimation affects behavioural fidelity before it affects feasibility, so confidence measures such as
\cref{eq:inverse_posterior} are valuable even when they do not alter the final ranking.

The online extension succeeds in both environments.
For the two non-ego players, the final inferred orders coincide with the ground truth in highway and city,
yielding a match rate of $1.0$ in both runs.
In highway, the resulting behaviour is comfortably feasible: the minimum clearance remains positive,
all local solves succeed, and the ego vehicle removes more than $92\%$ of its initial goal error.
The side vehicles also preserve substantial forward progress while yielding to the ego vehicle.

The city scenario is more constrained.
The minimum clearance becomes effectively active, and the minimum solve-success rate drops to $0.625$ for the
players that interact most strongly with the road boundaries.
Nevertheless, the inference layer still converges to the correct final orders, and the ego vehicle removes roughly
three quarters of its initial goal error.
Taken together with \cref{fig:inverse_online_trajectories}, these results show that inverse GOOP reasoning can be
embedded inside a receding-horizon controller without sacrificing the structure introduced in
\cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:inverse-discussion}

Preference-order estimation extends games of ordered preference beyond the complete-information assumption used by
current GOOP solvers.
The exhaustive estimator provides a transparent baseline, but it scales factorially with the number of objective
levels and can only discriminate through the next executed control.
The multi-step estimator enriches the comparison by incorporating short-horizon state and control consistency and by
maintaining a posterior over candidate orders, which helps diagnose when the inference problem is weakly
identifiable.
The adaptive online extension shows that the same ideas can be carried into closed-loop interaction, where order
estimation and control are updated together over time.

At the same time, the experiments reveal the present limits of the framework.
Inference remains sensitive to short-horizon ambiguities, as illustrated by the highway case,
and the online method relies on a hand-designed finite candidate set rather than the full permutation space.
Even with those limitations, the chapter demonstrates that inverse GOOPs can recover meaningful preference-order
information and use it to support decentralized interaction in scenarios where ordered objectives are not known a
priori.
