\chapter{Introduction}

\section*{Motivation}

Autonomous driving systems repeatedly face decisions that cannot be reduced to a
single scalar objective. In practice, an autonomous agent must simultaneously
reason about safety, traffic rules, comfort, and task completion. These
objectives often conflict, and the conflicts are contextual: what is considered a
reasonable trade-off in a highway setting may be unacceptable in dense urban
traffic. This creates a central modeling challenge: how should an autonomous
agent represent and compute decisions when some objectives have strict priority
over others?

Games of ordered preference (GOOPs) provide a principled formulation for this
setting by combining multi-agent interaction with lexicographic objective
hierarchies \cite{lee:2025}. Instead of assigning fixed scalar weights to all
objectives, GOOPs encode priority order directly. This makes the formulation
closer to rule-based driving requirements and to how human experts reason about
non-negotiable versus negotiable constraints.

The main downside is computational cost. As the number of agents, horizon
length, or preference levels increases, the induced optimization problems become
hard to solve online. Recent work has shown that approximate methods based on
iterated best response (IBR) can provide strong practical performance in this
regime \cite{delasheras:2025}. This thesis starts from that contribution and
extends it toward deployment and estimation.

\section*{From paper to thesis}

This master thesis is organized around four artifacts that represent successive
stages of the same research line:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The paper \emph{Approximate solutions to games of ordered preference},
    which introduces lexicographic IBR over time as an efficient approximate
    solver in receding horizon \cite{delasheras:2025}.
    \item The \texttt{patos} project, which integrates the paper's planning
    concepts into Duckietown robotics software, connecting optimization outputs
    to real-time robot control.
    \item The \texttt{InverseGoopSolver} project, which explores inverse and
    decentralized estimation workflows on top of the same GOOP library.
    \item An ongoing follow-up manuscript (targeting RA-L) focused on inference
    of preference relations from partial observations in interactive driving-like
    scenarios.
\end{enumerate}

The thesis objective is not only to summarize these artifacts independently, but
to connect them into a coherent progression:

\begin{center}
\emph{efficient forward solving $\rightarrow$ embodied multi-robot integration
$\rightarrow$ inverse preference estimation.}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement}

The broad problem addressed by this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
How can we make lexicographically structured multi-agent planning practical for
interactive autonomous systems when both \emph{computation} and \emph{information}
are limited?
\end{quote}

This broad problem decomposes into two practical bottlenecks:

\begin{description}
    \item[Computation bottleneck.] Exact or tightly coupled formulations of
    GOOPs can be expensive to solve in receding horizon, especially when
    preference hierarchies are deep and interactions are strongly coupled
    \cite{lee:2025}.
    \item[Information bottleneck.] In realistic environments, an agent does not
    directly observe the preference hierarchy of others. Even if a forward solver
    is efficient, using it in mixed-traffic settings requires some form of
    inverse estimation of hidden preference structure.
\end{description}

The first bottleneck is the main contribution of the published paper
\cite{delasheras:2025}. The second bottleneck motivates the ongoing extension
work in \texttt{InverseGoopSolver} and the follow-up manuscript.

\section*{Scope of this draft}

This document is intentionally written as a \emph{strong first master-thesis
draft}. Some work packages are still under active development, so the current
version emphasizes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item coherent formulation and notation,
    \item reproducible software architecture,
    \item clear methodology for upcoming experiments,
    \item transparent separation between completed and ongoing results.
\end{itemize}

Final quantitative comparisons for all extensions are not yet complete at the
time of writing. Therefore, this version includes preliminary discussion and an
explicit completion roadmap instead of definitive final conclusions.

\section*{Contributions of this thesis draft}

At this stage, the thesis contributes the following integrated narrative:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A unified presentation of GOOP modeling and lexicographic IBR over
    time, grounded in the published paper and adapted to thesis-level detail.
    \item A system-level description of how the method is bridged into a
    Duckietown control stack (ROS nodes, Julia solver bridge, state-feedback
    loop, and safety fallbacks).
    \item A structured report of inverse estimation prototypes that test
    centralized, decentralized, and preference-order identification workflows on
    top of the same planning core.
    \item A concrete plan to transition from software prototypes to final
    thesis-grade experimental evidence and final manuscript conclusions.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Positioning within current research}

This thesis sits at the intersection of three active lines of work:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{prioritized decision-making} in autonomous systems
    \cite{zanardi:2022,lee:2025},
    \item \textbf{efficient trajectory-game computation} in receding horizon
    \cite{hall:2022,hall:2024,laine:2023}, and
    \item \textbf{inverse multi-agent inference} from partial observations
    \cite{peters:2023,li:2023,liu:2024}.
\end{enumerate}

The core thesis claim is that these lines are more useful when treated as one
continuous pipeline rather than isolated topics: forward planning methods should
be designed with deployment constraints in mind, and deployment-ready systems
should naturally expose interfaces for inverse estimation.
