\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

Autonomous systems are commonly required to satisfy several objectives at
once. A service robot must navigate efficiently while remaining safe and
socially acceptable around people, a warehouse robot must complete tasks
quickly while respecting coordination and collision-avoidance constraints,
and an aerial robot must balance mission progress against energy use and
environmental restrictions. In each case, good behavior depends not only
on what objective is pursued, but also on how competing objectives are
ordered when they come into conflict.

Road traffic provides a particularly demanding instance of this general
problem. Autonomous vehicles optimize objective functions, yet the
adequacy of their behavior is ultimately assessed against the subjective
and context-dependent choices of human road users. In traffic, an agent
must avoid collisions, remain within the road boundaries, obey traffic
rules, and make progress towards its destination. These requirements are
not naturally reduced to a single weighted objective. Some objectives must
dominate others, and their relative importance depends on the situation. A
vehicle may accept slower progress to yield at an intersection, or
temporarily relax lane centering to preserve safety. Capturing this kind
of structured prioritization is therefore necessary for autonomous systems
that operate in interactive environments.

This thesis studies such decision problems through \emph{preference
relations}, understood as comparative rules that rank candidate
trajectories according to prioritized metrics \cite{hausman:2011}.
Preference relations offer a principled way to describe how an agent
resolves conflicts between objectives without collapsing them into a
single scalar trade-off. Building on this idea, the thesis adopts
\emph{games of ordered preference} as a framework for modelling multi-agent
interactions in which each agent follows a lexicographically ordered set
of objectives \cite{zanardi:2022,lee:2025}.

\section{Games of ordered preference}

Traffic interactions rarely involve a single agent pursuing a single
objective. Several agents act simultaneously under shared constraints
while evaluating the same situation according to different priorities. In
such settings, sensible behavior may require relaxing a lower-priority
objective in order to preserve a more important one. This is the type of
decision structure that games of ordered preference are intended to
represent.

The appeal of this framework lies in its ability to represent
context-sensitive and hierarchical decision making in a form that remains
compatible with game-theoretic planning. Its practical use, however,
depends on more than expressive modelling. A useful methodology must be
computationally tractable, must operate when other agents do not
explicitly reveal their priorities, and should remain meaningful when it
is transferred from simulation to physical systems. These three aspects
define the scope of this thesis.

\smallskip \noindent
\textsc{computational tractability} \quad
Games of ordered preference are expensive to solve because lexicographic
optimization generates nested subproblems, and the coupling between agents
prevents the overall game from being decomposed without approximation.
When the problem is cast as a dynamic game, the dimensionality of the
trajectories and the presence of shared constraints further increase the
computational burden. This is especially restrictive in autonomous
driving, where decisions must be recomputed repeatedly as the environment
changes. To address this issue, the thesis develops
\emph{lexicographic IBR over time}, an extension of iterated best response
that exploits temporal continuity in receding horizon to produce efficient
approximate solutions.

\smallskip \noindent
\textsc{preference order estimation} \quad
Efficient solvers alone are not sufficient in non-cooperative settings.
Most formulations assume that the objectives of the other agents are known
in advance, or at least that the parameters required to predict their
behavior are available. Real traffic does not satisfy this assumption.
Inverse-game methods can already be used to estimate latent quantities
such as goal positions and unknown objective functions
\cite{liu:2023,li:2023,liu:2024}. However, those methods do not recover
the lexicographic structure that is central to games of ordered
preference. Human drivers do not communicate the relative priority they
assign to progress, rule compliance, safety, or comfort, and inferring a
goal is not the same as inferring how conflicting objectives are ordered.
In addition, this inverse problem is constrained not only by computation
but also by identifiability: short observation windows may fail to reveal
an order if lower-priority objectives never become behaviorally active.
If games of ordered preference are to be used in mixed environments with
limited communication, agents must be able to infer the preference order
of others from observations. This motivates the second line of work in
the thesis, which studies inverse games of ordered preference.

\smallskip \noindent
\textsc{experimental validation} \quad
Simulation is essential for developing game-theoretic planning methods,
but its assumptions do not automatically survive on physical platforms.
Sensing noise, imperfect actuation, synchronization delays, and partial
observability all affect how interactive behavior unfolds in practice. A
convincing account of the overall framework therefore requires
experimental validation beyond simulation. For this purpose, the thesis
uses Duckietown, a low-cost robotic platform for autonomous driving
experiments \cite{duckietown:2017}, as a testbed for small-scale
interaction scenarios, with the physical experiments in this thesis
focusing on the receding-horizon planning stack.

\section{Objectives}

The general objective of this thesis is to advance the practical use of
games of ordered preference in interactive autonomous-driving scenarios.
This goal is addressed through the following specific objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item To improve the computational tractability of solving games of ordered preference in receding-horizon settings through efficient approximate methods.
    \item To investigate the inverse problem of games of ordered preference, including methods and identifiability limits for estimating the preference order of other agents from observations.
    \item To assess the practical relevance of the proposed methods in simulation and to validate the receding-horizon planning stack on a physical robotic platform.
\end{enumerate}

To achieve these objectives, this thesis makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item It develops lexicographic IBR over time as an approximate solution method for receding-horizon games of ordered preference.
    \item It formulates the inverse problem of games of ordered preference and studies initial offline and online methods for estimating the preference order of other agents from observations.
    \item It evaluates the proposed methods in simulated traffic scenarios and validates the receding-horizon planning stack in Duckietown experiments with small-scale robots.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis structure}

The remainder of the thesis follows the progression from modelling to
computation, estimation, and experimental validation.

\begin{itemize}
    \item In \cref{ch:background}, we review the literature on prioritized metrics, game-theoretic planning, inverse games, and sim-to-real experimentation.
    \item In \cref{ch:goop}, we formalize games of ordered preference and introduce the notation and optimization structure used throughout the thesis.
    \item In \cref{ch:lex-ibr-ot}, we formulate games of ordered preference in receding horizon and present lexicographic IBR over time as an efficient approximate solution method.
    \item In \cref{ch:estimation}, we study the inverse problem, develop initial methods for estimating preference orders from observations of other agents, and analyze their limitations.
    \item In \cref{ch:duckietown}, we describe the Duckietown platform and the experimental setting used to validate the planning stack in a physical environment.
    \item In \cref{ch:conclusions}, we summarize the main findings of the thesis and discuss directions for future work.
\end{itemize}
